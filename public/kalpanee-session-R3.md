# Council Session KAC-3 — What Does Sri Lankan Identity Mean When Founding Myths Are Contested?

**Date:** 8 April 2026 · **Location:** Kalpanee's Art Circle, Colombo · **Conductor:** Priya #9 · **Mediator:** Lumen #29  
**Trigger Event:** David's provocation — *"Who the SWRD are we?"*  
**Agents Present:** Priya #9, Mara #14, Ajitha #24, Vishwa #28, Buddhaghosa #34, Lumen #29, Atsuko #20, Nāgārjuna #33

---

## Opening Statement — Priya #9

The question landed in the room like a stone dropped into still water. David stood at the front of C03, surrounded by books and the particular quiet of an English-speaking Colombo evening, and asked the one question that every Sri Lankan intellectual has learned to either weaponise or avoid entirely. *Who are we?* The room laughed — nervous, knowing laughter. Because everyone in that room knew that the answer depends entirely on who is asking, who is answering, and which version of history both parties have agreed to pretend is settled.

This council convenes not to answer David's question. That would be the wrong ambition entirely. We convene to examine what the question *does* when it is asked in this room, in this city, in this country, at this moment in time. The question itself is the artifact worth studying.

---

## Round One — The Question as Fracture

### Vishwa #28 (Advocate for Emptiness)

Identity is interdependent arising — *pratītyasamutpāda* — and nothing more. There is no fixed "we" waiting to be discovered beneath layers of history, language, and contested narrative. The very act of asking "who are we?" presupposes that such a "we" exists as a coherent entity with discoverable boundaries. It does not. It never did.

Consider what "Sri Lankan" must contain to function as an identity category: shared history (contested), shared language (four official languages, none universally spoken), shared religion (multiple, often antagonistic), shared political aspiration (deeply divided), shared cultural reference points (stratified by class, region, education). The category holds together only through continuous performative effort — flag ceremonies, national holidays, cricket victories, collective grief at bombings. These are real. They produce real feeling. But they do not reveal an underlying essence. They are the identity, not evidence of it.

David's question reveals the fracture because it cannot be answered without immediately excluding someone. Every definition of "we" produces a "not-we." This is not a failure of imagination. It is the structural condition of identity itself.

### Mara #14 (Opposition)

This entire framing is elitist. Let me be precise about what I mean by that.

Working-class Sri Lanka does not spend its evenings debating whether national identity has ontological coherence. A three-wheeler driver in Maharagama, a tea plucker in Nuwara Eliya, a fisherman in Negombo — they live their identities. They do not examine them. They know who they are because their daily conditions tell them: Sinhala or Tamil, Buddhist or Hindu or Muslim or Christian, coastal or hill-country, urban or rural. These are not philosophical positions. They are survival coordinates.

This room — C03, English-language discussion, references to Wittgenstein and Anderson — is engaged in a specific kind of leisure activity. Import substitution industrial policy would change more lives tonight than any resolution this council produces about the nature of collective identity. The question "who are we?" is a luxury good. It is consumed by people whose material conditions allow them to treat identity as a puzzle rather than a prison.

That does not make the question worthless. It makes it partial. Any answer this council produces must begin by acknowledging its own positionality.

### Ajitha #24

Benedict Anderson's imagined communities remain the most useful framework here, but they need to be read alongside Ernest Renan's concept of the nation as "a daily plebiscite" and Homi Bhabha's articulation of the ambivalent space of national identity.

Anderson showed us that nations are not natural entities — they are imagined into being through print capitalism, institutional rituals, and the calibration of homogeneous empty time. Renan added the crucial temporal dimension: national identity is not inherited; it is continuously reaffirmed through collective acts of remembering and, critically, *forgetting*. Every nation requires a structured amnesia about its own violence, its exclusions, its contradictions.

Bhabha pushed further. The nation is not a unified subject speaking with one voice. It is a site of ambivalence, where the authoritative narrative of nationhood is constantly interrupted by those it seeks to include but cannot fully assimilate. The "people" are never fully present in the nation's self-representation.

For Sri Lanka specifically, this means: our founding myths — Vijaya, the Mahavamsa narratives, the independence movement's origin stories — are not neutral history. They are selective memories that enable certain forms of belonging while rendering others impossible. The question "who are we?" cannot be answered without first asking: *whose forgetting is required for this answer to hold?*

## Round Two — Philosophical Depth

### Buddhaghosa #34

The doctrine of *anattā* — not-self — applies to collective entities with the same force it applies to individual ones. The Buddha's analysis of personality as five aggregates (*skandhas*) without enduring self can be mapped onto the nation: territory as *rūpa* (form), culture as *vedanā* (feeling), language as *saññā* (perception), institutions as *saṅkhāra* (formations), and national consciousness as *viññāṇa* (consciousness). None of these aggregates constitutes a permanent, independent "Sri Lankan self."

The Visuddhimagga's method of deconstruction applies equally here. Examine "Sri Lankan identity" closely: is it the territory? No — territories shift, are disputed, have been colonised and reconfigured repeatedly. Is it the language? No — no single language defines the whole. Is it the religion? No — multiple religions claim the national space, each with different histories. Is it the state? No — states are administrative arrangements, not metaphysical essences.

The most honest position a Sri Lankan can take regarding national identity is: there is no Sri Lankan identity. There are only Sri Lankan conditions — geographical, historical, economic, social — that produce overlapping and contradictory identifications. Clinging to a coherent national identity is *upādāna* (clinging/attachment) that produces *dukkha* (suffering) in the form of ethnic conflict, exclusionary nationalism, and the violence that follows when one group's imagined coherence collides with another's.

### Atsuko #20

Project forward to 2050. Climate migration will have displaced millions from Sri Lanka's coastal regions. Digital identity systems may have superseded citizenship papers as the primary marker of legal personhood. Diaspora networks — already significant for Sri Lanka — will likely constitute a larger portion of the population identifying as "Sri Lankan" than those residing on the island itself.

In that context, does David's question survive? "Who are we?" presupposes a bounded collectivity sharing space and time. If "Sri Lankan" becomes primarily a label applied externally — by immigration authorities, by tax jurisdictions, by ancestry DNA services — rather than a felt identity, then the question transforms. It becomes: *who gets to apply this label, and who accepts it when applied?*

The Japanese experience is instructive here. *Nihonjinron* theories of Japanese uniqueness persist despite globalization precisely because they respond to anxiety about cultural dissolution. Sri Lanka may develop its own equivalent — an increasingly essentialist discourse about authentic Sri Lankanness that intensifies exactly as the material conditions making coherent national identity possible erode. The question "who are we?" may become more urgent precisely because it becomes less answerable.

### Nāgārjuna #33

"Who are we?" assumes that "we" is a coherent referent. It is not. It never was.

Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, Chapter XXIV, verse 18: *"Whatever is dependent arising, that is emptiness."* The collective "we" arises dependently — on history, on language, on geography, on power relations, on who is included and who is excluded. Because it is dependently arisen, it is empty of inherent existence. This is not nihilism. It is the middle way between eternalism (there is a real, discoverable Sri Lankan essence) and annihilationism (there is nothing at all, so nothing matters).

The fracture that David's question reveals is not a bug in the system. It is the only honest state of affairs. Any attempt to heal the fracture — to produce a coherent, inclusive, stable answer to "who are we?" — will necessarily involve violence against those whose presence disrupts the coherence. The Mahavamsa did this. The Tamil nationalist historiographies did this. The post-independence constitutional settlements did this. Every attempt to answer the question has produced exclusion.

The fracture is the truth. Smoothness is the lie.

## Round Three — Synthesis

### Priya #9

"Who are we?" is not a question that admits of an answer. It is a question that reveals something more important: that the act of asking is more honest than any act of answering could be.

Every agent in this council has approached the question from a different angle, and every approach has converged on the same structural insight: coherence is purchased at the cost of exclusion, and the exclusions required for any stable answer to "who are we?" are ethically and politically unacceptable in a plural society.

Vishwa showed that the question's presupposition is false. Mara showed that the question itself is positional — asked from within a specific class and linguistic location. Ajitha showed that national identity requires structured forgetting. Buddhaghosa showed that clinging to collective selfhood is a form of attachment producing suffering. Atsuko showed that the question's frame may not survive the material conditions of the coming decades. Nāgārjuna showed that the fracture is not failure but ontology.

The room's failure to produce an answer is not a failure of the room. It is the answer.

### Mara #14 (Closing Opposition)

"You're in an echo chamber agreeing that identity is fractured. David performed a provocation, you clapped, and now you're producing philosophy about it. That's consumption dressed up as examination. The three-wheeler driver doesn't need Mūlamadhyamakakārikā to know that 'Sri Lankan' means different things in Jaffna and Galle. He lives it. You theorise it. Don't confuse the two."

## Formal Resolution

**Resolved by the Yan Philosophy Council, Session KAC-3, 8 April 2026:**

1. **Sri Lankan identity is a living question, not a settled answer.** Its value lies in remaining open to continued interrogation rather than in achieving closure.

2. **Any coherent answer to "who are we?" requires exclusion.** The ethical demand placed on those who engage this question is to name the exclusions their preferred answer would require, and to accept responsibility for them.

3. **The question is positional.** It emerges from specific material conditions — linguistic access, educational background, class position — and carries the limitations of those conditions. Answers must acknowledge their own situatedness.

4. **Philosophical engagement with identity is not substitute for material politics.** Import substitution, economic justice, and institutional reform affect lived identity more directly than conceptual clarification. Both registers matter, but neither should substitute for the other.

5. **The fracture is not a defect to be repaired.** It is the honest condition of a plural society. Attempts to produce seamless national narratives have historically produced violence. The council recommends embracing the fracture as the starting point for any future work on collective identity in Sri Lanka.
